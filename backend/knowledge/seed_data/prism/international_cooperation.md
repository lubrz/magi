# International Space Cooperation

The International Space Station (ISS) represents the most successful international scientific collaboration in history — 15 nations contributing modules, crew, and funding since 1998. The ISS proved that geopolitical rivals (US-Russia) can cooperate in space even during terrestrial tensions. However, the ISS model also reveals cooperation's limits: it took 13 years to assemble, cost over $150 billion, and decision-making was slowed by consensus requirements. The Artemis Accords (2020) offer a different model — a US-led framework with bilateral agreements rather than multilateral consensus. China's exclusion from ISS (by US Congressional mandate) drove them to build their own Tiangong station, fragmenting international space cooperation into competing blocs.
